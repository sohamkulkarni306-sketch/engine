    <span class="eyebrow">Page 02 — Evolution</span>
    <h1 style="font-size:clamp(2.4rem,6vw,4.6rem);">FROM ONE<br>CYLINDER TO <em>SIXTEEN</em></h1>
    <p class="hero-sub">Every extra cylinder is a different answer to the same question: how do you turn
      controlled explosions into smooth, usable power? Walk the timeline the count on the left is not
      decoration, it's the whole story.</p>
  </div>
</header>

<section class="section">
  <div class="wrap">
    <div class="timeline">

      <!-- I4 -->
      <div class="tl-item reveal">
        <div class="tl-cyl mono">I4</div>
        <div class="tl-card">
          <span class="tag">Where it all scaled up</span>
          <h3>Inline-Four</h3>
          <div class="tl-grid">
            <div>
              <p>Four cylinders in a single straight row sharing one crankshaft. It's the simplest way to build
                a "real" multi cylinder engine cheap to manufacture, compact, and smooth enough for everyday
                driving. It became the default engine for the world's economy cars, hot hatches and everyday
                commuters, and it's the layout most people learn to drive on without ever knowing its name.</p>
              <p>Because it's narrow and short, it fits transversely under a hood with room to spare which is
                exactly why it became the backbone of mass-market motoring through the 20th century.</p>
            </div>
            <div>
              <figure class="figure">
                <img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Buda_four_cylinder_engine_(1920).jpg" alt="Early inline-four cylinder engine">
                <figcaption>An early production inline-four cylinder engine block</figcaption>
              </figure>
              <div class="spec-list" style="margin-top:14px;">
                <div><b>Cylinders</b><span>4 in a row</span></div>
                <div><b>Character</b><span>Efficient, compact, affordable</span></div>
                <div><b>Found in</b><span>Hatchbacks, sedans, most motorcycles</span></div>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- V4 -->
      <div class="tl-item reveal">
        <div class="tl-cyl mono">V4</div>
        <div class="tl-card">
          <span class="tag">Compact power, uneven heartbeat</span>
          <h3>V4</h3>
          <div class="tl-grid">
            <div>
              <p>Take a four cylinder engine and fold it into a narrow "V" instead of a straight line, and you get
                something dramatically shorter ideal when space is tight. V4s are rarer in cars than in
                motorcycles, where Honda's VF/VFR line popularized the layout in the early 1980s for its
                compactness and near perfect primary balance at a 90° bank angle.</p>
              <p>The tradeoff is character: depending on the crank design, a V4 can fire unevenly, giving it a
                distinctive, lumpy exhaust note that racers and riders have come to love rather than tolerate.</p>
            </div>
            <div>
              <figure class="figure">
                <img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/2000_Honda_VFR800_V4_engine_with_right_crankcase_cover_removed.jpg" alt="Honda VFR800 V4 engine">
                <figcaption>Honda VFR800 90° V4 engine, crankcase cover removed</figcaption>
              </figure>
              <div class="spec-list" style="margin-top:14px;">
                <div><b>Bank angle</b><span>Commonly 90°</span></div>
                <div><b>Character</b><span>Narrow, distinctive uneven note</span></div>
                <div><b>Found in</b><span>Sport motorcycles, select sports cars</span></div>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- V6 -->
      <div class="tl-item reveal">
        <div class="tl-cyl mono">V6</div>
        <div class="tl-card">
          <span class="tag">The practical middle ground</span>
          <h3>V6</h3>
          <div class="tl-grid">
            <div>
              <p>The V6 became the workhorse "step-up" engine of the automotive world. Short enough to fit where
                a straight six never could, it offered more power and a smoother delivery than a four cylinder
                without the length penalty of an inline six. The Lancia Aurelia is widely credited as the first
                series-production car to use one, in 1950; Buick's 1962 V6 helped popularize the layout in
                America.</p>
              <p>By the 1990s, V6 had overtaken straight six as the most common six cylinder configuration on
                the road the sensible choice between economy and muscle.</p>
            </div>
            <div>
              <figure class="figure">
                <img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/3100_V6_Engine.JPG" alt="A production V6 automobile engine">
                <figcaption>A production 60° V6 automotive engine</figcaption>
              </figure>
              <div class="spec-list" style="margin-top:14px;">
                <div><b>Bank angle</b><span>Typically 60° or 90°</span></div>
                <div><b>Character</b><span>Balanced, versatile, compact</span></div>
                <div><b>Found in</b><span>Sedans, SUVs, sports coupes</span></div>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- V8 -->
      <div class="tl-item reveal">
        <div class="tl-cyl mono">V8</div>
        <div class="tl-card">
          <span class="tag">The sound of American muscle</span>
          <h3>V8</h3>
          <div class="tl-grid">
            <div>
              <p>Eight cylinders in two banks of four, usually at a 90° angle a layout that, with the right
                crankshaft design, achieves near perfect primary balance while producing that unmistakable deep,
                rolling exhaust burble. The V8 became the emotional center of muscle cars, pickup trucks and
                classic hot rods, prized as much for its character as for its raw torque.</p>
              <p>It remains the layout most people picture the instant someone says the word "engine" big,
                confident, and endlessly tunable by generations of home mechanics.</p>
            </div>
            <div>
              <figure class="figure">
                <img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Ford_v8_custom_engine_1.JPG" alt="A Ford V8 engine">
                <figcaption>A classic Ford derived V8 automobile engine</figcaption>
              </figure>
              <div class="spec-list" style="margin-top:14px;">
                <div><b>Bank angle</b><span>Usually 90°</span></div>
                <div><b>Character</b><span>Deep, rumbling, high torque</span></div>
                <div><b>Found in</b><span>Muscle cars, trucks, V8 supercars</span></div>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- V12 -->
      <div class="tl-item reveal">
        <div class="tl-cyl mono">V12</div>
        <div class="tl-card">
          <span class="tag">Silk instead of thunder</span>
          <h3>V12</h3>
          <div class="tl-grid">
            <div>
              <p>Twelve cylinders means twelve tiny, overlapping power pulses instead of a handful of big ones
                which is why a V12 doesn't shove you back in your seat so much as pour power in, smoothly, all
                the way to a screaming redline. It's the signature engine of Ferrari and Lamborghini flagships,
                classic Jaguars, and the wartime Rolls Royce Merlin aircraft engine that powered the Spitfire.</p>
              <p>V12s are expensive, heavy and thirsty to build which is exactly why they've become a symbol
                of automotive prestige rather than a practical daily choice.</p>
            </div>
            <div>
              <figure class="figure">
                <img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Ferrari_V12_engine.jpg" alt="A Ferrari V12 engine">
                <figcaption>A naturally aspirated Ferrari V12 engine</figcaption>
              </figure>
              <div class="spec-list" style="margin-top:14px;">
                <div><b>Bank angle</b><span>Commonly 60°</span></div>
                <div><b>Character</b><span>Silky, high revving, refined</span></div>
                <div><b>Found in</b><span>Ferrari, Lamborghini, Rolls Royce flagships</span></div>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- W16 -->
      <div class="tl-item reveal">
        <div class="tl-cyl mono">W16</div>
        <div class="tl-card">
          <span class="tag">The outlier two V8s fused together</span>
          <h3>W16</h3>
          <div class="tl-grid">
            <div>
              <p>Sixteen-cylinder production engines are almost unheard of the rare exception is the Volkswagen
                Group's 8.0-litre quad-turbo W16, built exclusively for the Bugatti Veyron and Chiron since 2005.
                It's effectively two narrow-angle VR8 engines joined at a 90° angle on a shared crankshaft, four
                turbochargers force feeding all sixteen cylinders at once.</p>
              <p>In its most extreme Bugatti Bolide tune, the W16 produces roughly 1,825 horsepower a figure
                that made hypercar top speeds of 300+ mph possible for the first time. Bugatti retired the W16
                in 2024 after the Chiron Super Sport L'Ultime, closing out one of the strangest chapters in
                engine history.</p>
            </div>
            <div>
              <figure class="figure">
                <img loading="lazy" decoding="async" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Volkswagen_W16.jpg" alt="Bugatti Veyron W16 quad-turbo engine">
                <figcaption>The Bugatti/Volkswagen 8.0L quad turbo W16</figcaption>
              </figure>
              <div class="spec-list" style="margin-top:14px;">
                <div><b>Configuration</b><span>Two VR8 banks at 90°</span></div>
                <div><b>Forced induction</b><span>Four turbochargers</span></div>
                <div><b>Found in</b><span>Bugatti Veyron &amp; Chiron only</span></div>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

    </div>
